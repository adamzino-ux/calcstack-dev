How to Use This Debt Payoff Calculator

Enter each debt you owe — including the current balance, annual interest rate, and minimum monthly payment. You can add as many debts as you have. Then enter any extra monthly amount you can put toward debt payoff, choose your strategy (avalanche or snowball), and click Calculate. You'll instantly see your debt-free date, total interest cost, and a complete month-by-month schedule.

Why This Matters

The average American household carries over $96,000 in debt. Without a clear payoff plan, most people pay tens of thousands of dollars in unnecessary interest — and stay in debt years longer than they need to.

Consider this: a $10,000 credit card balance at 22% APR with a $250 minimum payment takes over 5 years to pay off and costs nearly $5,800 in interest. Adding just $100/month extra cuts that to 3 years and saves $2,400. That's real money back in your pocket.

Whether you're tackling student loans, credit cards, a car loan, or medical bills, knowing your exact payoff timeline gives you a target to work toward. The avalanche method (highest interest first) minimizes total interest — perfect if you want to optimize mathematically. The snowball method (smallest balance first) gives you quick wins that keep motivation high — great if you've struggled to stay on track before.

How It's Calculated

Each month, interest is applied to the remaining balance, then your payment reduces the principal:

Monthly Interest = Balance × (Annual Rate / 12 / 100)
Principal Paid = Payment − Monthly Interest
New Balance = Balance − Principal Paid

With multiple debts, minimum payments are made on all debts, and any extra payment is applied entirely to the target debt (highest interest for avalanche, lowest balance for snowball). When one debt is paid off, its former minimum payment is rolled into the next target debt — this "debt rollover" is what makes these strategies so powerful.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Don't skip the minimum payments. Paying less than the minimum damages your credit score and triggers penalty fees. Always include your true minimums.
  • Even $50 extra makes a big difference. Small additional payments dramatically reduce total interest over time — use the slider to see the impact instantly.
  • Re-run after a payoff. As debts close, update the calculator with your current balances for the most accurate timeline.
  • Include all debts. It's tempting to ignore a small debt, but including it ensures the rollover math works correctly and you capture every dollar of savings.
  • Don't confuse APR with monthly rate. Credit card APR is annual — this calculator automatically converts it to a monthly rate for accurate results.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between avalanche and snowball?

The avalanche method targets the highest interest rate debt first, which minimizes total interest paid. The snowball method targets the lowest balance first, which gives you faster "wins" and can help motivation. Mathematically, avalanche always wins financially, but snowball works better for people who need psychological momentum to stay on track.

How much does an extra payment really help?

Significantly. On a $15,000 debt at 18% APR, an extra $100/month can cut 2+ years off your timeline and save over $3,000 in interest. The earlier in the repayment you add extra payments, the more you save. Use the extra payment slider above to see your exact savings.

Should I pay off debt or invest?

If your debt interest rate is higher than your expected investment return (typically 6–8% for a diversified stock index), paying off debt first is the better mathematical choice. High-interest credit card debt (15–25%) almost always beats investing. Low-interest debt like a mortgage (3–6%) is a closer call, and many people do both simultaneously.

What if I can't afford the minimum payments?

Contact your creditors — many have hardship programs, deferment options, or can lower your minimum temporarily. You can also explore debt consolidation (rolling multiple debts into one lower-rate loan) or speak with a nonprofit credit counseling agency. Never just stop paying without a plan, as this damages your credit severely.
