How to Use This Mortgage Payment Calculator

Enter your home price (or loan amount), down payment, annual interest rate, and loan term. The calculator instantly shows your monthly principal & interest payment plus an optional breakdown including property tax and insurance (PITI). You can also enter an extra monthly payment to see how much interest you'd save and how many months you'd shave off your loan.

Use the sliders to quickly explore different interest rate and loan amount scenarios. The amortization table shows exactly how your balance decreases year by year.

Why This Matters

Your mortgage is almost certainly the largest financial commitment of your life. A 0.5% difference in interest rate on a $350,000 loan over 30 years means a difference of roughly $35,000 in total interest paid. That's a car. Understanding your payment before you commit is non-negotiable.

Consider a couple buying their first home at $400,000 with 10% down ($360,000 loan). At 6.5%, their monthly P&I payment is $2,276. If they stretch to a $450,000 home, that same rate bumps the payment to $2,554 — a difference of $278/month, or $3,336/year. Over a decade, that's $33,360 more out of pocket before even counting interest.

Real estate agents and sellers will tell you what you can afford. This calculator tells you what you'll actually pay. Use it to compare 15-year vs. 30-year terms, shop competing rates, and decide how much extra you can throw at the principal each month to become mortgage-free faster.

How It's Calculated

The standard mortgage payment formula uses compound interest amortization:

M = P × [r(1+r)ⁿ] / [(1+r)ⁿ - 1] Where: M = monthly payment P = principal loan amount (home price − down payment) r = monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12) n = total number of payments (years × 12)

For example: $280,000 loan at 6.5% for 30 years → r = 0.065/12 = 0.005417, n = 360. M = 280,000 × [0.005417 × (1.005417)³⁶⁰] / [(1.005417)³⁶⁰ − 1] ≈ $1,770/month.

Each month, interest is calculated on the remaining balance. Early payments are mostly interest; later payments are mostly principal. The amortization table shows this shift year by year.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Don't forget PITI: Your real monthly housing cost includes property taxes, homeowners insurance, and possibly PMI if your down payment is under 20%. This calculator shows that total.
  • PMI alert: With less than 20% down, expect to add $50–$200/month in private mortgage insurance until your equity reaches 20%.
  • Extra payments are powerful: Paying just $200/month extra on a $350,000, 30-year loan at 6.5% can save over $80,000 in interest and cut 6+ years off your loan.
  • Compare 15 vs. 30 year: A 15-year mortgage typically offers a lower rate and you'll pay dramatically less interest overall — but the monthly payment is significantly higher. Use this calculator to see both scenarios side-by-side.
  • Rate shopping matters: Even a 0.25% lower rate is worth pursuing. On a $400,000 loan, that's roughly $18,000 over 30 years.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's included in a mortgage payment?

A basic mortgage payment covers principal (reducing your loan balance) and interest. The full "PITI" payment also includes property taxes and homeowners insurance, often held in escrow by your lender. If your down payment is under 20%, PMI is typically added as well.

Should I choose a 15-year or 30-year mortgage?

A 30-year mortgage gives you a lower monthly payment and more cash flow flexibility, but you'll pay significantly more interest over the life of the loan. A 15-year mortgage costs more each month but builds equity faster and saves tens of thousands in interest. If you can comfortably afford the higher payment, the 15-year is usually the better financial choice.

How does my credit score affect my mortgage rate?

Your credit score is one of the biggest determinants of your interest rate. Borrowers with scores above 760 typically qualify for the best rates, while scores below 640 may face rates 1–2% higher or difficulty qualifying at all. Improving your score before applying can save you thousands over the life of the loan.

What is an amortization schedule?

An amortization schedule shows how each payment is split between interest and principal over the life of your loan. In the early years, the majority of each payment goes to interest. As your balance decreases, the interest portion shrinks and more of each payment pays down the principal. This calculator shows a yearly summary of this schedule.
