You can also account for vacation days and public holidays for a more accurate "true hourly" rate. Hit Calculate and you'll see your exact hourly wage along with every pay period equivalent.

Why This Matters

Most job offers list an annual salary — but your real take-home experience depends on how many hours you actually put in. A $70,000/year job at 40 hours/week works out to $33.65/hour. But if that role regularly requires 50-hour weeks, your real hourly rate drops to $26.92. That 25% gap is the difference between two jobs that look identical on paper.

This calculator is especially useful when comparing job offers across different employment types. A contractor quoting $50/hour versus an employee earning $95,000/year — which is better? The answer depends on hours, benefits, and days off. Freelancers also use this tool in reverse: knowing their target hourly rate helps them price projects correctly.

Another common use case: overtime planning. Salaried employees exempt from overtime who consistently work 55+ hours are effectively earning far less per hour than they realize. Seeing that number — say $22/hour for a "well-paid" $65,000 role — can be eye-opening and a powerful tool in salary negotiations.

How It's Calculated

The core formula converts any salary period to an annual figure first, then divides by total hours worked:

Hourly Rate = Annual Salary ÷ (Hours Per Week × Weeks Per Year)

Weeks per year is adjusted by subtracting vacation and holidays: Effective Weeks = 52 − (Vacation Days + Holiday Days) ÷ 5. This gives you the "true" hourly rate reflecting only days you're actually paid to work. All other periods (monthly, weekly, daily) are derived from this base annual figure.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Don't assume 52 weeks: Most full-time workers take some vacation. Use your actual weeks worked for an honest hourly equivalent.
  • Include unpaid overtime: If you regularly work 45–50 hours but are paid a fixed salary, adjust the hours/week slider up to see your true rate.
  • Contractors need a buffer: A $50/hour contractor rate looks equivalent to a $104,000 salary — but contractors pay self-employment tax, fund their own benefits, and have gaps between clients. Aim for 1.3–1.5× the equivalent employee rate.
  • Don't forget benefits: Health insurance, 401(k) matching, and paid time off add 20–35% to compensation. A $65,000 salary with full benefits can exceed $85,000 in total value.
  • Use bi-weekly for paycheck planning: Most employers pay bi-weekly (26 paychecks/year). Check your bi-weekly gross to budget monthly expenses accurately.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many work hours are in a year?

A standard full-time employee works 40 hours/week × 52 weeks = 2,080 hours per year. If you account for 10 vacation days and 10 federal holidays, that drops to roughly 1,920 hours. Use the sliders in the tool to match your exact situation.

What's the hourly rate for a $50,000 salary?

At 40 hours/week with 52 weeks worked, a $50,000 annual salary equals $24.04/hour. With 2 weeks vacation (50 working weeks), it rises to $25.00/hour since you're paid the same amount for fewer hours worked.

How do I convert hourly to salary?

Multiply your hourly rate by hours per week, then by weeks worked per year. For example: $25/hour × 40 hours × 52 weeks = $52,000/year. This calculator does the reverse, but the same formula applies both ways.

Is overtime included in salary calculations?

This tool calculates your base effective hourly rate. If you're a salaried employee who works overtime without additional pay, increase the "Hours Per Week" input to see your true effective hourly rate. Hourly employees typically earn 1.5× their base rate for hours over 40/week.
