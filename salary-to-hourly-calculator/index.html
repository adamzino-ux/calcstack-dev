How to Use This Salary to Hourly Calculator

Select your salary period (annual, monthly, or weekly), then enter your salary amount. Adjust the hours per week and weeks per year sliders to match your actual schedule. Optionally, enter unpaid vacation time to get your true effective hourly rate. Hit Calculate to instantly see your hourly wage alongside a full pay period breakdown.

Why This Matters

Understanding your real hourly rate goes far beyond a simple curiosity — it's a practical tool for making smarter financial decisions. Say you're comparing two job offers: one pays $80,000 per year with the expectation of 50 hours per week, and another offers $72,000 with a strict 40-hour week. The first role sounds better by $8,000, but its hourly rate works out to roughly $30.77/hr versus $34.62/hr for the lower-paying job. You'd actually earn more per hour at the lower-salaried position.

This also matters for freelancers setting their rates. If you previously earned $65,000 as an employee (with benefits, paid vacation, and employer taxes), you shouldn't quote $31/hr as a freelancer — after accounting for self-employment tax, no paid time off, and no benefits, you'd be earning significantly less in real terms.

Teachers, healthcare workers, and salaried professionals often work unpaid overtime. A nurse on a $75,000 salary working 50 hours a week earns just $28.85/hr — not the $36/hr that 40-hour math suggests. Knowing this number gives you leverage in salary negotiations and clarity on your actual compensation.

How It's Calculated

The core formula is straightforward:

Hourly Rate = Annual Salary ÷ (Hours per Week × Weeks Worked per Year)

Weeks Worked = Weeks per Year − Unpaid Vacation Weeks

Example: $75,000 ÷ (40 hrs × 52 weeks) = $75,000 ÷ 2,080 = $36.06/hr

If you enter a monthly or weekly salary, it's first converted to an annual figure: monthly × 12 or weekly × 52. All pay period breakdowns (daily, biweekly, etc.) are then derived from this annual figure using standard working day and week counts.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Don't assume 40 hours. If you regularly work 45–50 hours, use your real number — the difference adds up to thousands of dollars in "invisible" unpaid wages.
  • Account for unpaid time off. Two weeks of unpaid vacation reduce your working year to 50 weeks, raising your effective hourly rate slightly — but your annual take-home is the same.
  • Freelancers: add a 30–40% premium. Your hourly rate as a freelancer needs to cover self-employment tax (~15.3%), no paid benefits, and unbillable hours for admin and marketing.
  • Salary vs. total compensation. Remember that salary is just one part of your comp. Employer 401(k) matching, health insurance, and bonuses can add 20–30% on top of base pay.
  • Use the right period tab. If your contract states a monthly salary, use the Monthly tab — don't manually multiply and risk rounding errors.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many working hours are in a year?

A standard full-time year is 2,080 hours (40 hours/week × 52 weeks). However, if you subtract 10 federal holidays and 2 weeks of paid vacation, you're looking at approximately 2,000 hours. The exact number depends on your employer and schedule, which is why this calculator lets you customize it.

What's the hourly rate for a $50,000 salary?

At 40 hours per week for 52 weeks, a $50,000 annual salary equals $24.04 per hour. If you only work 50 weeks (accounting for 2 weeks off), it rises slightly to $25.00/hr because the same salary is spread across fewer working hours.

Should I include overtime when calculating my hourly rate?

For salaried employees, overtime typically isn't paid separately — your salary covers all hours worked. This is exactly why entering your real hours per week matters: if you work 50 hours on a $60,000 salary, your effective rate is $23.08/hr, not $28.85/hr. Use your actual average weekly hours for the most accurate result.

How do I calculate an hourly rate from a monthly salary?

Switch to the "Monthly" tab and enter your monthly gross pay. The calculator multiplies it by 12 to get an annual figure, then divides by your total working hours per year. For example, $5,500/month × 12 = $66,000 ÷ 2,080 hours = $31.73/hr.
