Why This Matters

When you work for an employer, your payroll taxes are split 50/50 — you pay 7.65% and your employer pays 7.65%. As a self-employed person (freelancer, contractor, sole proprietor, LLC member), you're both employee and employer. That means you owe the full 15.3% on your net earnings, up to the Social Security wage base ($168,600 in 2024). Above that, you still owe the 2.9% Medicare tax — and if your income exceeds $200,000 ($250,000 married filing jointly), an additional 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax kicks in.

For someone earning $80,000 from freelance work, this means roughly $11,304 in SE tax alone — before any income tax. Many new freelancers are blindsided by this bill come April. Knowing your SE tax liability upfront lets you set aside the right amount each quarter, avoid underpayment penalties (typically triggered when you owe more than $1,000), and plan deductions like a SEP-IRA contribution to reduce your taxable income. The one bright spot: you can deduct 50% of your SE tax from your adjusted gross income, effectively lowering your income tax bill.

How It's Calculated

The IRS uses a specific formula to calculate self-employment tax:

  • Step 1: Multiply net SE income × 92.35% — this is your "net earnings from self-employment" (the 7.65% deduction approximates the employer-side deduction)
  • Step 2 — Social Security tax: Net earnings × 12.4%, up to the SS wage base ($168,600 for 2024). If you have W-2 wages, that amount reduces the available SS base.
  • Step 3 — Medicare tax: Net earnings × 2.9% (no wage cap). If net earnings exceed the Additional Medicare Tax threshold, an extra 0.9% applies to the excess.
  • Step 4 — Deductible half: Total SE tax ÷ 2. This is an above-the-line deduction from gross income.
  • Formula: SE Tax = (Net Income × 0.9235 × 0.124) + (Net Income × 0.9235 × 0.029)

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Don't forget quarterly payments: SE tax must be paid quarterly (April 15, June 17, September 16, January 15). Missing these triggers underpayment penalties even if you pay in full by April.
  • Deduct the right business expenses first: Your SE tax is based on net income. A $10,000 home office deduction doesn't just reduce income tax — it reduces your SE tax base too, saving you ~15.3%.
  • SEP-IRA can dramatically lower your bill: Contributing to a SEP-IRA lets you deduct up to 25% of net SE income (after the SE deduction), directly reducing taxable income.
  • Health insurance premiums are deductible: If you pay your own health insurance, 100% of premiums are deductible from adjusted gross income (not just as a Schedule A itemized deduction).
  • Watch the Social Security wage base: If you have both W-2 employment and freelance income, your W-2 wages count against the SS cap first, potentially reducing your SE Social Security tax.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the self-employment tax rate for 2024?

The SE tax rate is 15.3% on net earnings up to $168,600 (12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare). Above $168,600, only the 2.9% Medicare portion applies. High earners above $200,000 ($250,000 MFJ) also owe an additional 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax on income above those thresholds.

Can I deduct self-employment tax on my return?

Yes — you can deduct exactly half of your SE tax as an above-the-line adjustment to income on Schedule 1 of Form 1040. This deduction is automatic and doesn't require itemizing. It reduces your adjusted gross income, which in turn lowers your federal (and often state) income tax.

Do I owe SE tax if I only made a small amount?

You owe SE tax if your net self-employment income is $400 or more in a year. Below $400, you're not required to file Schedule SE. However, even a small income is worth reporting if you want to build Social Security credits — you need 40 credits (roughly 10 years of work) to qualify for retirement benefits.

Is self-employment tax different from income tax?

Yes, they're completely separate taxes. Self-employment tax (Schedule SE) covers your Social Security and Medicare obligations. Income tax (Form 1040) is calculated on your taxable income and depends on your bracket, deductions, and credits. You owe both, which is why effective tax rates for self-employed people are often higher than for W-2 employees at the same gross income level.
