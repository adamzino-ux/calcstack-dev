Why This Matters

Understanding your amortization schedule is one of the most powerful things you can do before signing a loan. Here's a scenario most people don't realize: on a $300,000 mortgage at 7% for 30 years, you'll pay roughly $418,000 in interest alone — more than the original loan. That's $718,000 total for a $300,000 asset.

But add just $200/month in extra payments and you'll shave off about 5 years and save over $75,000 in interest. The amortization schedule shows exactly why: in the early years, nearly 80% of each payment goes to interest, not principal. Knowing this helps homebuyers, car buyers, and student loan borrowers decide whether to refinance, make extra payments, or choose a shorter term.

This tool is used by first-time homebuyers running "what-if" scenarios, financial advisors showing clients the true cost of debt, and borrowers deciding between a 15-year and 30-year mortgage.

How It's Calculated

Loan amortization uses a fixed-payment formula where each payment covers the current period's interest first, then reduces the principal. The standard formula for the fixed periodic payment is:

M = P × [r(1+r)^n] / [(1+r)^n − 1]

Where: M = payment per period, P = principal (loan amount), r = periodic interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12 for monthly), n = total number of payments (years × 12 for monthly).

For each period: Interest = Remaining Balance × r, Principal = M − Interest, New Balance = Remaining Balance − Principal Paid. Extra payments reduce the balance faster, shortening the loan term.

Bi-weekly payments use the same logic but divide the annual rate by 26 and apply 26 payments per year, which naturally results in one extra monthly-equivalent payment per year.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Don't confuse APR with interest rate. APR includes fees; the interest rate is what drives the amortization math. Use the stated interest rate, not APR, in this calculator.
  • Even small extra payments make a big difference early on. An extra $100/month in year 1 saves far more than $100/month in year 20 because the balance is much larger early.
  • Bi-weekly is not the same as twice a month. True bi-weekly = 26 payments/year = 1 extra monthly payment per year, which meaningfully shortens your loan.
  • This calculator covers principal & interest only. Your actual mortgage payment likely includes property taxes, insurance, and PMI — budget for those separately.
  • Refinancing math: Use this tool to compare your current loan balance vs. a new rate/term to see whether refinancing saves you money after accounting for closing costs.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is an amortization schedule?
An amortization schedule is a table showing every payment over the life of a loan, broken down into how much goes to interest vs. principal, and the remaining balance after each payment. It helps you understand exactly how your debt is paid off over time and how much of each payment builds equity.
Why is so much of my early payment interest?
Because interest is calculated on the outstanding balance, which is at its highest at the start of the loan. As you pay down principal, the interest portion shrinks and the principal portion grows — this is called negative amortization in reverse. After about 60% of your loan term, you finally pay more principal than interest each period.
Does making extra payments change my monthly payment?
In most standard loans, no — your required monthly payment stays the same. Extra payments go entirely toward reducing principal, which shortens the loan term and reduces total interest paid. Some loans require you to specify that extra funds go to principal; always check with your lender.
What's the difference between monthly and bi-weekly payments?
Monthly payments = 12 per year. Bi-weekly payments = 26 per year (every 2 weeks), which is the equivalent of 13 monthly payments annually. That one extra payment per year can reduce a 30-year mortgage by 4–6 years and save tens of thousands in interest.
