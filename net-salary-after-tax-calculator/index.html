How to Use This Net Salary After Tax Calculator

Enter your gross annual salary, choose your filing status and state, then add any pre-tax deductions like 401(k) contributions or health insurance premiums. Hit "Calculate Net Salary" to instantly see your take-home pay broken down by year, month, biweekly period, and week.

Why This Matters

Your gross salary and your actual take-home pay can differ dramatically. Someone earning $85,000 in California might pocket just $57,000 after federal, state, and FICA taxes — a difference of over $28,000. That gap has real consequences: it determines whether you can afford a mortgage, how aggressively you can save, and whether a job offer in a different state is actually worth taking.

Consider a common scenario: you're offered a $100,000 job in New York vs. a $90,000 job in Texas. After taxes, the Texas salary nets roughly $72,000 while the New York role nets about $65,000 — making the lower nominal salary the better financial deal. This calculator surfaces those hidden differences so you can make informed comparisons.

Pre-tax contributions matter too. Putting $10,000/year into a 401(k) doesn't reduce your take-home by $10,000 — because that contribution lowers your taxable income, your federal and state tax bills shrink, meaning the true after-tax cost might be closer to $6,500–$7,500 depending on your bracket.

How It's Calculated

The calculator uses 2024 federal income tax brackets with the standard deduction applied first ($14,600 single / $29,200 married / $21,900 head of household). Your pre-tax deductions (401k, health insurance) further reduce taxable income before brackets are applied.

Formula:

  • Taxable Income = Gross Salary − Standard Deduction − Pre-Tax Deductions
  • Federal Tax = Sum of marginal bracket taxes on Taxable Income
  • Social Security = min(Gross, $168,600) × 6.2%
  • Medicare = Gross × 1.45% (+ 0.9% on income over $200k)
  • State Tax ≈ Gross × Effective State Rate (simplified flat-equivalent)
  • Net Salary = Gross − Federal Tax − SS − Medicare − State Tax − Pre-Tax Deductions

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Don't forget FICA: Social Security (6.2%) and Medicare (1.45%) together take 7.65% of every dollar — many people focus only on income tax and are surprised by their actual paycheck.
  • Pre-tax beats post-tax for current savings: Maxing your 401(k) ($23,000 in 2024) at a 22% bracket saves $5,060 in federal taxes alone — free money left on the table otherwise.
  • State taxes vary wildly: Moving from California (13.3% top rate) to Texas (0%) can mean an extra $10,000+ per year at higher salaries with no change in gross pay.
  • This is an estimate: Actual withholding depends on your W-4 allowances, local city taxes, and specific state progressive brackets — consult a CPA for tax planning.
  • Bonus and side income are taxed separately: Bonuses are often withheld at a flat 22% federal rate and stack on top of your salary for bracket purposes at year-end.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between gross salary and net salary?

Gross salary is what your employer pays you before any deductions — it's the number on your offer letter. Net salary (take-home pay) is what actually hits your bank account after federal taxes, FICA (Social Security + Medicare), state taxes, and any pre-tax benefit deductions are removed. For most Americans, take-home pay is 65–80% of gross salary.

How accurate is this calculator?

This tool uses 2024 federal tax brackets with the standard deduction and simplified state effective rates. It's accurate for straightforward W-2 employees using the standard deduction. It does not account for local/city taxes, itemized deductions, tax credits, investment income, or alternative minimum tax. For complex situations, consult a tax professional.

Does a 401(k) contribution really lower my taxes?

Yes — traditional 401(k) contributions are pre-tax, meaning they reduce your taxable income dollar-for-dollar before federal and most state income taxes are calculated. On a $75,000 salary in the 22% federal bracket, contributing $6,000 to a 401(k) saves you $1,320 in federal taxes alone. Your actual paycheck reduction is less than the contribution amount.

Why do I owe more taxes than expected at filing time?

Paycheck withholding is an estimate based on your W-4 elections. If you have multiple jobs, significant investment income, freelance work, or claim fewer allowances than your actual situation, you can end up underpaying throughout the year and owe a balance come April. Updating your W-4 with your employer or making estimated quarterly payments can prevent this.
