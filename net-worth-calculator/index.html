Why This Matters

Net worth is the single most important number in personal finance — more telling than income. A person earning $200,000 per year with $400,000 in debt is actually less financially secure than someone earning $60,000 who has been steadily building assets for a decade.

Knowing your net worth lets you track real progress. Someone who pays down $10,000 in student loans while contributing $5,000 to a 401(k) increased their net worth by $15,000 — but their paycheck didn't change. Tracking net worth monthly or annually reveals the true trajectory of your financial life.

According to the Federal Reserve, the median US household net worth is around $192,000 — but that figure varies wildly by age. People in their 30s typically have a net worth under $100,000, while those approaching retirement often exceed $400,000. Your goal isn't to beat a median — it's to trend upward every year.

This calculator is also useful for estate planning, applying for loans, or simply setting financial goals. Many financial advisors recommend calculating your net worth at least once per year, ideally at the same time (New Year's Day is popular).

How It's Calculated

The formula is beautifully simple:

Net Worth = Total Assets − Total Liabilities

Assets include: cash, checking/savings accounts, investments, retirement accounts (401k, IRA, Roth), real estate market value, vehicles (current resale value), and valuables like jewelry or art.

Liabilities include: mortgage balances, car loans, student loans, credit card balances, personal loans, medical debt, and any other amounts owed.

Note: use current market value for assets (not what you paid), and outstanding balance for liabilities (not the original loan amount). This gives the most accurate snapshot of your financial position today.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Use current market value for property: If you bought your home for $300,000 but it's worth $420,000 today, enter $420,000 as the asset — and enter your remaining mortgage as the liability.
  • Don't forget retirement accounts: A 401(k) or IRA counts as an asset even if you can't access it penalty-free yet. It represents real wealth.
  • Include vehicle depreciation: Cars lose value rapidly. Check Kelley Blue Book for a realistic current resale estimate, not your purchase price.
  • Be honest about credit card debt: Include every card balance. Underestimating liabilities is the most common mistake that inflates a false sense of security.
  • Recalculate annually: Track your net worth year-over-year to measure real progress. A $10,000 increase per year means roughly $1M more over a 40-year career.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a "good" net worth?
A good benchmark is to multiply your age by your gross annual income and divide by 10. At 35 earning $80,000, a target net worth would be $280,000. That said, starting later or recovering from setbacks doesn't mean you can't build wealth — the direction of change matters more than the current number.
Should I include my home as an asset?
Yes — enter the current estimated market value as an asset, and your remaining mortgage balance as a liability. The difference (your home equity) is what contributes to your net worth. Just be aware that home equity is illiquid; you can't easily spend it without selling or borrowing against it.
How often should I calculate my net worth?
Most financial experts recommend once per quarter or at minimum once per year. Checking too frequently (like weekly) can cause anxiety from market fluctuations that don't reflect real progress. Annual reviews give a clear, noise-free picture of your financial trajectory.
Can my net worth be negative?
Absolutely, and it's very common — especially for recent graduates with student loans or young families with a new mortgage. A negative net worth just means your liabilities currently exceed your assets. The goal is to trend it upward over time through saving, investing, and debt paydown.
